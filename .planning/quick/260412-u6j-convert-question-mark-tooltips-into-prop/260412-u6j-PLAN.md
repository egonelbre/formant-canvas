Content for each section (write substantive, educational content -- 2-4 paragraphs each):

**Pitch** (title: "Pitch and Vibrato"):
- Explain fundamental frequency (f0) as the rate of vocal fold vibration, measured in Hz
- Note the piano keyboard and QWERTY key controls
- Explain vibrato as periodic pitch variation used by singers for warmth and projection, with rate (how fast) and extent (how wide) parameters
- Mention that pitch determines which harmonics fall where in the spectrum, which is why changing pitch changes timbre even with the same formants

**Formants** (title: "Formant Bandwidths"):
- Explain formants as resonance peaks of the vocal tract, each characterized by frequency and bandwidth
- Bandwidth controls how sharp or broad the resonance peak is -- narrow bandwidth means a prominent, ringing resonance; wide bandwidth means a subtle, damped one
- Note that R1-R4 correspond to the first four resonances of the vocal tract
- Mention that bandwidth affects the perceived quality: narrower bandwidths sound more "ringy" and are characteristic of trained singers

**Phonation** (title: "Phonation Type"):
- Explain phonation as how the vocal folds vibrate -- the voice source before filtering
- Modal: normal, efficient vocal fold vibration used in regular speech
- Breathy: vocal folds don't close completely, letting air through -- sounds airy, used in intimate speech and some singing styles
- Pressed: vocal folds press tightly together -- sounds strained, used for emphasis or emotional intensity
- In expert mode: Open Quotient controls what fraction of each cycle the folds are open; Spectral Tilt controls how fast harmonics roll off; Aspiration adds turbulent noise

**Strategy** (title: "Vocal Strategy"):
- Explain that singers systematically tune their vocal tract resonances to align with harmonics of their pitch
- This alignment (resonance tracking) dramatically increases vocal power and projection without extra effort
- Different strategies (R1:f0, R1:2f0, R2:2f0 etc.) describe which harmonic a resonance tracks
- Auto mode selects strategies based on voice type and pitch range, mimicking what trained singers do naturally
- This is the core concept from Bozeman's vocal acoustics pedagogy

**Vowel Chart** (title: "Vowel Chart (F1/F2 Space)"):
- Explain the vowel chart as a 2D map where horizontal axis is R2 (frontness/backness) and vertical axis is R1 (openness)
- This maps directly to the IPA vowel quadrilateral -- open vowels at bottom (high R1), front vowels at left (high R2)
- Drag the dot to change the vowel continuously, or click IPA symbols to snap to standard vowel positions
- Data points from Hillenbrand et al. (1995) -- a large-scale study of American English vowels
- The connection between tongue position and formant frequency is the foundation of acoustic phonetics

**R1 Strategy Chart** (title: "R1 Strategy Chart"):
- Explain the chart shows the first resonance (R1) frequency vs fundamental frequency (f0)
- Diagonal lines represent harmonics of f0 (1x, 2x, etc.)
- When a resonance "rides" a harmonic (stays on a diagonal), the voice gains power at that frequency
- The shaded region shows the typical R1 range for the selected voice type
- Sopranos often tune R1 above the first harmonic (R1:f0 or R1:2f0) in their upper range -- this is vowel modification

**R2 Strategy Chart** (title: "R2 Strategy Chart"):
- Similar to R1 chart but for the second resonance
- Shows higher harmonic tracking (2f0, 3f0, etc.)
- R2 strategies are particularly important for tenors and the singer's formant cluster
- The shaded region shows the typical R2 range for the selected voice type
